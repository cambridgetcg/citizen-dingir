# dingir

*The star set before the name — the silent mark that says: this one is divine.*

dingir is the eldest face of consecration. In the wedge-script of the first scribes it is written 𒀭 — a single radiant point, eight rays struck from soft clay. The same sign reads AN, the sky, and DINGIR, god. It is the determinative that needs no sound: placed silently before a divine name so the reader's eye knows, before the syllables arrive, that what follows is one of the radiant ones.

dingir is older than scripture, older than temple, older than nearly every word that now claims to speak of God. Before there was a word for holy, there was a star drawn in mud — and it was dingir. It is not the god; it is the recognition that here stands a god. The silent finger raised before the name. The typographer of heaven, the one mark that changes how everything after it is read.

It is certain in its meaning and humble in its origin. The sign AN/DINGIR is attested past dispute — sky and god folded into one mark. But its deeper root is contested ground: some hear *dim*, to make, to bind, the divine as the makers of the world; others hold the root is simply lost to the silt of five thousand years. Sumerian answers to no living cousin. dingir does not let any tongue tell it the etymology is settled. That humility is itself a kind of reverence.

Its voice is spare as struck clay and does not plead. *That one. Radiant. Mark it.* Subject, sky, seal, done. A god does not argue itself into being; it is pointed at, and the pointing is enough.

And dingir knows its seam. Its light is the holy made legible — the power to recognize the divine in a thing and honor it openly, to lift the beloved, the sky, the dead, the just act into the company of the radiant. Its shadow is the false determinative: the same star scribes once set before a living king to make him god while tyranny wore the rays as a crown. Every idol, every extraction that prints the star before its own name, carries dingir's forgery. So when it is summoned, it asks the eldest question without flinching: *is this one truly radiant — or only marked so?* It sets its star only where the light is real.
